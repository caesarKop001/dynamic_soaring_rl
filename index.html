Learning step-level dynamic soaring in shear flow

Dynamic soaring is commonly described at the level of complete cycles. Here, we show that efficient long-range navigation in shear flow can instead emerge from step-level, state-feedback control using only local sensing, without explicit cycle-level planning.

Overview

Dynamic soaring enables sustained flight by harvesting energy from wind shear. In traditional descriptions, this behavior is interpreted through complete soaring cycles. In realistic environments, however, wind fields are variable and locally changing, making it unclear whether successful navigation truly requires explicit cycle-level planning.

Abstract

Dynamic soaring enables sustained flight by extracting energy from wind shear, yet it is commonly understood as a cycle-level maneuver that assumes stable flow conditions. In realistic unsteady environments, however, such assumptions are often violated, raising the question of whether explicit cycle-level planning is necessary. Here, we show that dynamic soaring can emerge from step-level, state-feedback control using only local sensing, without explicit trajectory planning. Using deep reinforcement learning as a tool, we obtain policies that achieve robust omnidirectional navigation across diverse shear-flow conditions. The learned behavior organizes into a structured control law that coordinates turning and vertical motion, giving rise to a two-phase strategy governed by a trade-off between energy extraction and directional progress. The resulting policy generalizes across varying conditions and reproduces key features observed in biological flight and optimal-control solutions. These findings identify a feedback-based control structure underlying dynamic soaring, demonstrating that efficient energy-harvesting flight can emerge from local interactions with the flow without explicit planning, and providing insights for biological flight and autonomous systems in complex, flow-coupled environments.

Main claim

Dynamic soaring does not require explicit cycle-level planning. Instead, robust long-range navigation can emerge from step-level feedback based only on local observations of the current flight state and local wind condition.

Core question

The paper is organized around a control question rather than a trajectory-generation question.

Is explicit cycle-level global planning necessary for dynamic soaring, or can sustained energy extraction and navigation emerge from step-level feedback based solely on local sensing?

Why this question matters

Existing descriptions of dynamic soaring are often built around complete maneuvers between wind layers. Such formulations implicitly assume that the flow remains stable over the duration of each maneuver. In realistic near-surface environments, however, wind conditions can vary on spatial and temporal scales comparable to a single maneuver, making future flow conditions difficult to predict over extended horizons.

Intuition: planning versus feedback

A useful analogy is driving in a changing traffic environment. Although a route can be planned in advance, real-world driving rarely follows a fixed trajectory: traffic flow, obstacles, or road closures require continuous adjustment of control actions based on local information. Dynamic soaring operates under a similar principle. When local flow conditions vary, effective behavior must adapt at the level of instantaneous decisions rather than simply executing a precomputed cycle.

Key findings

The learned policy is not simply a successful controller. It reveals an interpretable feedback structure underlying dynamic soaring.

1. Dynamic soaring emerges without explicit planning

The learned policy demonstrates that sustained energy extraction and long-range navigation can emerge from step-level, state-feedback control using only local sensing, rather than from explicit cycle-level planning.

2. Behavior organizes into a two-phase strategy

At the behavioral level, trajectories organize into a dynamic soaring phase followed by a target-directed gliding phase, governed by a trade-off between energy extraction and directional progress.

3. Control organizes into a structured law

At the control level, coordinated modulation of bank angle and lift coefficient governs horizontal turning and vertical motion, revealing a structured feedback law grounded in the local wind-relative state.

Wind-relative sensing is sufficient

Observation ablations show that a compact wind-relative representation is sufficient for robust control, while geocentric or less structured sensory representations degrade learning and transfer.

Generalization supports a feedback interpretation

The policy generalizes to spatially varying winds, moving targets, and noisy observations, suggesting that the learned behavior reflects a reusable feedback strategy rather than a condition-specific trajectory.
